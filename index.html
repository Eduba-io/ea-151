The Opening

Where the Chamber already stands on AI.

The Technology / Innovation Committee is carrying the Chamber's work into the AI era. The AI for Small Business session in November, the Smart Cities series, and the Chamber's seat at the South Florida ClimateReady Tech Hub all point in the same direction. Member businesses are asking the next question now, which is how to move from interest to operating practice. The posture needed is a program, not another panel.

That is where Eduba fits. Eduba is an AI consulting and training firm run by Jake Van Clief, a Marine Corps veteran and University of Edinburgh graduate whose methodology has been published in ACM TiiS. The framework is simple and measurable: in most business problems, about 60% of the work belongs in traditional code and databases, about 30% in rule-based logic, and about 10% in AI. Teams that skip that distinction waste budget putting language models where Postgres would finish the job faster. Teams that get it right spend less and ship more.

The Fit

Three gaps this program is shaped to close.

01 · Programming

From event to cohort.

SMB members sitting in the AI for Small Business workshop walk out with enthusiasm and no operating framework. A six-to-eight week cohort converts enthusiasm into practice on real workflows the member brought in the door.

02 · Workforce

The layer between MDC and the hiring side.

MDC's Miami Tech Works trains the workforce entering Miami jobs. Member employers need the workforce they already have AI-ready now. That middle layer is open, and the Chair's dual role at MDC makes the alignment tight.

03 · Signature

A Chamber-branded AI readiness credential.

The Chamber's advantage is 118 years of convening power. A published methodology (ICM, ACM TiiS) under a Chamber-branded cohort lifts the programming above "another vendor workshop" and gives the Committee something citable.

What It Looks Like

A Chamber-branded cohort, in practical terms.

Weeks 01 & 02

Intake on the 60 / 30 / 10 frame.

Each member company sends two or three people. They bring real workflows from their business. We map what belongs in code, what belongs in rules, and what belongs in AI.

Weeks 03 & 04

Tool stack and governance.

Working sessions on the tools member businesses actually use, with a short governance layer suited to Florida-regulated industries. No vendor pitches.

Weeks 05 & 06

Prototype one workflow.

Each company ships a working prototype for one workflow they mapped in week 1. Scoped deliverables, not slideware.

Weeks 07 & 08

Demo day and alumni network.

Graduates present to the Committee. The Chamber owns the alumni list. Thirty-day follow-up measures adoption and hours saved.

Scope language rather than price tags sits on this page by design. The Chamber's programming typically runs through member underwriting or co-branded sponsorship, and the first conversation should be about structure, not a line-item invoice.

The Methodology

Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM) organizes agent context as a layered filesystem with measurable interpretability and reproducibility gains. Published in ACM TiiS, released under MIT license, with a 52-member practitioner community.

For Chamber programming it provides something rare in AI training: a citable, academically-reviewed frame that committee chairs can put in reports and that member companies can hand to their boards.
